UX Research / Case Study

Navigation Deficiencies
in Apple Maps

Adding custom route building to improve user navigation before and while driving.

Role
Lead UX Researcher & Designer
Methods
User Interviews, Competitive Analysis, Heuristic Evaluation, Usability Testing
Tools
Figma
Type
UX Case Study
Overview

The Problem with Apple Maps

Apple Maps launched in 2012 as a Google Maps competitor and fell short in significant ways. As a former courier and daily iPhone user, I depended on Maps for work and experienced its routing failures firsthand. Near-wrecks and lost wages from poor route control were not edge cases. They were regular occurrences.

Two specific failures drove this project: the inability to create multi-stop routes, and the severely limited "Add a Stop" feature. Every other major mapping application had solved this. Apple hadn't.

Research

Validating the Problem

Before designing anything, I reviewed hundreds of App Store user reviews and found consistent, specific complaints about route control, especially from delivery workers.

"The 'Add a Stop' feature is basically useless. It greatly limits the type of locations you can add... If I'm needing to stop at multiple places, it would be nice to add all the locations I want and be shown the most efficient circuit." (App Store reviewer)

App Store reviews: consistent, specific complaints about route control from delivery workers and everyday drivers

Competitive Analysis

Apple Was the Outlier

I conducted a competitive analysis across Google Maps, MapQuest, Here Maps, and Waze. All three offered custom route building with multiple user-defined stops. Apple was the only major navigation app that had not solved this.

Problem Definition

Finding the Right Question

A heuristic evaluation surfaced additional issues but I kept scope tight. The core problem was clear:

How might we allow users to plan their directions more efficiently before going on a trip and while driving?

I interviewed 5 Apple Maps users to validate this framing. Key findings:

  • 100% wanted the ability to add a custom stop not curated by Apple
  • 100% were frustrated by the inability to build dynamic multi-stop routes
  • "It just feels like I'm only being fed marketing stuff rather than it's actually about my route planning." (interviewee)
  • "There is no way to add a stop, unless you just cancel the route and redo it again." (interviewee)
Design

From Sketches to Prototype

I built two prototype flows: one for planning a trip before driving, one for adding a custom stop while driving. This kept the two problems isolated and testable independently.

Early sketches revealed a scope problem. I was trying to redesign the entire routing experience when the fix was surgical. Apple's add-a-stop feature already existed. It just lacked customization. The solution was extending what was already there, not replacing it.

Story map: two user flows, planning before a trip vs. adding a stop while driving

Usability Testing

Testing and Iteration

Lo-fi usability tests with 5 participants revealed one consistent issue: the call-to-action buttons were inconsistent with Apple's existing UI patterns, creating confusion about what would happen when tapped.

  • 100% of users flagged the CTA placement and icon choices as unintuitive
  • 100% understood the scenarios and felt the solution addressed the core problem
  • 80% completed all tasks without issue
  • "I think it's good. It feels better than Google in that there isn't a bog in menu options." (tester)

Hi-fidelity addressed the CTA issues and brought the UI into full alignment with Apple's design language.

Hi-fidelity prototype, Scenario 1: pre-trip multi-stop route with custom Add a Stop

Learnings

What I Took Away

The most important lesson: the visual fix was micro, not macro. I nearly wasted time redesigning a feature Apple had already solved. The real problem was a CTA change and a customization option added behind it. Staying at the right zoom level is a skill that takes practice to build.

Not every UX methodology adds value to every project. Doing a process to check a box is not research. The constraint of a focused scope forced better decisions, not fewer.

As of this writing, Apple has shipped custom route building in Maps. The fact that they eventually solved the same problem validates the direction of this work.
