Pre-meeting notes for Kari Goin Kono

Getting through pdx.edu when you are trying to finish your degree

This started from my own experience: switching from a BA in Graphic Design to a BS, and trying to work out what I actually needed to graduate. Four things tripped me up. All four are still live on the site this week, and I have linked each one so you can see it yourself. I would value your read as someone who does equity-centered UX for a living.

Lukas Green · findings re-checked against live pdx.edu on 2026-07-22

The one idea underneath all four findings

The content mostly exists. The problem is that it is scattered across duplicate pages and dense navigation tabs, so a student cannot get a straight answer to a normal question. I should be able to type the specific thing I want, "change my degree from a BA in Graphic Design to a BS," and land in one place that answers it: the steps, the requirements, and ideally a view that reads my own DARS audit and transcript to map the credits I have left. Instead I hunt through tabs, or an adviser tells me to "look on the website" and sends a link that is itself hard to navigate.

The north star: a search-first, filterable, centralized path finder that leans on the search bar and a student's own record, not on menus with thirty-plus links. The four findings below are the four places today's site forces the hunt instead.

Finding 1 · Redundant pages

Changing your major is one task split across two pages

To change my major I had to use two pages that overlap. The rule I most needed to see lives on only one of them, so if you arrive at the other first, you never learn it.

Live today · 2026-07-22
pdx.edu/advising/exploring-changing-majors Explains how to change a major
  • States the 90-credit declaration rule
  • Carries the Graphic Design / Architecture "portfolio review, once a year" rule
  • Links out to another page to actually do it
pdx.edu/registration/major-change Actually changes the major
  • Repeats the same 90-credit rule
  • Step 1 is "Log in to myPSU," with no warning it is behind a login
  • Never mentions the portfolio-review rule
pdx.edu/advising/change-your-major -> 404

Declare or change your major

  • Every constraint in one place: 90-credit deadline and the portfolio-review windows

  • The guessed URL redirects here instead of 404ing

Finding 2 · Search and findability

I cannot search for the specific thing I want

The most natural move, typing what I want into the search bar, fails. And when an adviser is busy, some just say "look on the website" and send a link, so the site is the front line, not a fallback. It has to answer a plain question, and it does not.

Finding 3 · Requirements to graduate

What it takes to finish your degree lives in six places

The question I most needed answered, "what do I actually need to complete this degree," has no single home. Even the official degree map splits it into six buckets, and there is a different map for every catalog year.

Finding 4 · Overloaded navigation

The tabs carry too much to read, especially for older learners

Open the "Student Life" tab on the homepage and roughly thirty-five links appear at once, in small type, with no hierarchy that helps you choose. I can read it because my eyesight is fine. An adult learner, an older student, or anyone with low vision cannot, and that population is a big part of who this case study is about. The answer is not a denser tab. It is fewer, merged categories plus a search bar that actually works.

Why I read this as an equity issue, not just a tidiness one

None of these fail an automated accessibility scan, and all of them fail a person. They tax the students carrying the most load: working adults, older and returning learners, first-generation students, anyone without an adviser to call or the eyesight to skim a wall of small links. A scattered journey quietly costs the people with the least time and the least slack. Merging concepts, leaning on search, and building one centralized place to answer "what do I need" is the fix I keep coming back to.

What I would love your read on

  1. Do these four match what you see on university sites, or am I missing a bigger pattern underneath them?
  2. Is a search-first, centralized path finder (fed by a student's own DARS and transcript) realistic here, or does the governance model make merging tabs the more achievable win?
  3. The overloaded menus read as a real accessibility and cognitive-load problem for older and low-vision learners. Does that match what you see, and how would you test it?
  4. The site sits with University Communications and OIT Site Dev. Who is the right person to hear a student-journey finding like this, and how does something like this usually get picked up inside PSU?
