The method

You don't erase your mistakes. You learn to read them.

The whole method lives in one exercise. Draw a circle freehand. Draw it again. Never erase a line. Every wrong stroke stays on the page, because a wrong line is information: it tells you where not to go.

Follow enough of them and you close in on a near-perfect circle built entirely out of your own mistakes. It's a drawing lesson and a life lesson wearing the same clothes.

Content matters. But it's the connection that counts.

That reframe is why this reaches people other programs can't. No art experience required. The lessons are self-directed, so the work keeps going long after the facilitator leaves the room.

Keep scrolling. It finds the circle.

Kirk drew these by hand, thousands of times, in a cell with one pencil. The wrongs are the point.

The twelve lessons

A sequence, on purpose.

Each lesson teaches a drawing skill and a way of thinking at the same time. You work them in order, at your own pace, with whatever you have.

The artist

I'm not a success story. I'm a working artist.

I did close to twenty years, the last eight at Eastern Oregon Correctional Institution. I drew the whole time. Eventually I started teaching what I'd figured out, and the room filled up.

In 2022 Portland Community College's Paragon Gallery gave my work a solo show, My Personal Experience — over forty pencil-and-pen drawings of life behind bars. I've since put the method into a book so it can go where I can't.
